## Reading List

Papers with AI summaries, ordered by relevance to project:

Cranmer et al. (NeurIPS 2020) — "Discovering Symbolic Models from Deep Learning with Inductive Biases" is the most important prior work. They train GNNs on N-body simulations with different force laws, encourage sparse latent representations in the message-passing module, then apply symbolic regression to the learned edge functions to extract analytical force laws. The key finding is that the symbolic expressions extracted from the GNN actually generalize better out-of-distribution than the GNN itself. This is essentially your project, but with MLP-based GNNs instead of KANs — and their code is public at github.com/MilesCranmer/symbolic_deep_learning.
Your value-add over Cranmer et al.: You'd swap the MLP edge functions for KAN edge functions. The KAN's spline activations are already in symbolic form, meaning you bypass the separate symbolic regression step — the learned functions are directly inspectable. This is a genuine architectural novelty in this specific application.

"Unveiling the Actual Performance of Neural-based Models for Equation Discovery on Graph Dynamical Systems" (arXiv 2508.18173) is the most recent and directly relevant work. They introduce the Graph KAN-ODE (GKAN-ODE), which applies KAN layers within a graph neural ODE framework specifically for equation discovery on graph dynamical systems. They also enhance the KAN architecture with multiplicative nodes to better capture multiplicative relationships common in physical dynamics (like F = ma or gravitational force). Their code is public and the benchmarks are reproducible. This paper appeared in August 2025 and is essentially in the same niche — so your work would need to differentiate from it, likely by focusing specifically on constrained systems (meshes, spring networks) or by leaning harder on the interpretability angle.


Lemos et al. (2023, Mach. Learn.: Sci. Technol.) extended the Cranmer approach to real solar system data, training on 30 years of NASA ephemeris data and then applying PySR symbolic regression to the learned edge function, recovering Newton's law of gravitation along with the relative masses of the bodies — including the gravitational constant — purely from observed trajectories. This is the "applied" version of what Cranmer demonstrated on simulations.
Thangamuthu et al. (NeurIPS 2022) — "Unravelling the Performance of Physics-Informed GNNs" is an important benchmark paper. They compare multiple physics-informed GNN architectures (HGNN, LGNN, GNODE and their constrained variants) on n-pendulums, n-springs, 4-body gravitational systems, and elastically deformable 3D solids, evaluating both trajectory accuracy and zero-shot generalizability to larger system sizes. This is basically the benchmark suite you'd want to replicate for your comparison.

"Discovering Symbolic Laws Directly from Trajectories" (HGNN paper, arXiv 2307.05299) presents a Hamiltonian GNN that decouples kinetic and potential energies and applies symbolic regression to recover the underlying energy functionals from trajectory data for n-springs, n-pendulums, gravitational systems, and binary Lennard-Jones systems. The Hamiltonian framing is a strong inductive bias you might want to consider adding to your Graph KAN.
Dynami-CAL GraphNet (Nature Comms, 2025) is the most recent mainstream entry: a physics-informed GNN that enforces pairwise conservation of linear and angular momentum by using edge-local reference frames that are equivariant to rotational symmetries and invariant to translations. This is your strongest baseline if you want to benchmark against a state-of-the-art physics-constrained GNN.

KAN 2.0 (Liu et al., 2024) is the canonical reference for why KANs are interesting for physics. The paper demonstrates KANs' capability to discover conserved quantities, Lagrangians, symmetries, and constitutive laws, and introduces MultKAN (with multiplication nodes) and a kanpiler that can compile symbolic formulas back into KAN form.
KAN-ODEs (Koenig et al., CMAME 2024) applies KAN layers inside neural ODEs for learning dynamical systems and hidden physics — a close relative to your work that doesn't use graph structure.
KA-GNNs for molecular property prediction (Nature Machine Intelligence, 2025) applies KAN layers within graph neural networks specifically for molecular property prediction, demonstrating improved accuracy and interpretability in scientific domains. This is in chemistry rather than classical mechanics, but the architecture is directly analogous.
