\section{Problem Statement}

In their 2020 paper, \textit{Discovering Symbolic Models from Deep Learning with Inductive Biases},
Cranmer et al.\cite{cranmer2020discoveringsymbolicmodelsdeep} introduced a deep learning method for extracting relationships from simulated physics
interactions. Their work involved training a graph neural network (GNN) on the $n$-body problem then
performing symbolic regression to recover a generalizable analytical form for Newton's law of universal gravity.

One limitation of Cranmer et al.'s\cite{cranmer2020discoveringsymbolicmodelsdeep} approach is that the GNN is not directly interpretable. The additional symbolic regression step is computationally expensive, and the quality of the obtained analytical form
depends on whether the deep latent representation is semantically meaningful, as the learned parameters
may not correspond to real physical relationships between features (eg. difference in velocity).

In 2024, Liu et al. demonstrated the potential of the Kolmogorov-Arnold network (KAN) to model physical
relationships in \textit{KAN} \cite{liu2024kankolmogorovarnoldnetworks} and \textit{KAN 2.0} \cite{liu2024kan20kolmogorovarnoldnetworks}. A KAN leverages the Kolmogorov-Arnold
representation theorem, which states that any multivariate function can be represented as a composition of univariate functions. While a traditional MLP has a fixed multivariate activation function at each node,
a KAN learns a univariate activation function for each edge \cite{liu2024kankolmogorovarnoldnetworks}. Liu et al.\cite{liu2024kankolmogorovarnoldnetworks}\cite{liu2024kan20kolmogorovarnoldnetworks} showed that KANs can outperform
MLPs in various cases and discussed the benefits of the function composition model in terms of interpretability.

Our goal is to explore the potential of a hybrid Kolmogorov-Arnold graph neural network (GKAN) architecture,
which replaces the multi-layer perceptron (MLP) layers in a GNN with KANs. We aim to prove this concept by training a GKAN on the
$n$-body problem following a similar approach as Cranmer et al.\cite{cranmer2020discoveringsymbolicmodelsdeep}, then evaluating the hybrid model against the
original GNN approach. We hypothesize that the GKAN can achieve comparable accuracy with the additional benefit
of being directly interpretable without requiring a symbolic regression step. We hope that this work contributes
to the ongoing research surrounding interpretability of deep models, specifically in the domain of physics discovery.

\section{Proposed Approach}

We will be using deep learning methods---specifically, we will focus on GNNs, KANs, and GKANs. In particular, we will implement a basic graph convolutional network (GCN) architecture (similar to Cranmer et al.'s\cite{cranmer2020discoveringsymbolicmodelsdeep} approach) and a base KAN architecture with b-spline activation functions \cite{liu2024kankolmogorovarnoldnetworks}, and perhaps explore variations of KANs such as the MultKan architecture \cite{liu2024kan20kolmogorovarnoldnetworks} if time permits. We will implement the GKAN by replacing the MLP layers in the GNN with KAN layers.

We will focus the domain of the problem to the $n$-body problem, which is a classical physics problem that involves predicting the trajectories of $n$ bodies interacting with each other under the influence of physical forces. The input to our model will be the initial conditions of the system (e.g., positions, velocities, masses), and the output will be the predicted trajectories of the bodies over time. We will generate our own data by simulating the $n$-body problem using a numerically symplectic ODE integrator such as leapfrog.

We will evaluate our approach using quantitative metrics
such as training/validation loss, as well as qualitative metrics such as interpretability, and by comparing our model against the baseline set by Cranmer et al.\cite{cranmer2020discoveringsymbolicmodelsdeep}. Furthermore, we will perform experiments with different ``gravity'' functions as well, \textit{hopefully} ablating the performance of the GKAN with different levels of model complexity and sparsity, to see if we can further improve the performance of the GKAN and understand the relationship between model complexity, interpretability, and generalization performance.

In general, we aim to follow the design set by Cranmer et al.\cite{cranmer2020discoveringsymbolicmodelsdeep} quite closely, as we want to evaluate
whether the transition to KAN layers is a meaningful improvement compared to MLP-based approaches. As we
continue to develop and iterate on the approach, we may explore other variations of the architecture, such as different KAN layer designs or ``feature hypothesis'' approaches \cite{liu2024kan20kolmogorovarnoldnetworks}, to see if we can further improve the performance of the GKAN.

Our project is primarily an ``application bake-off'' (GKANs vs. GNNs), though it incorporates elements of a few other project types (ie. ``New application'', ``Generalization to new setting'').

Ideally, we will show that GKANs outperform GNNs with symbolic regression as a method of recovering physical relationships, both in terms of accuracy and interpretability. We expect this to be the case
because the Kolmogorov-Arnold representation theorem framing allows us to choose our inductive biases
in a more physics-informed way, and the composition of univariate functions learned by the KAN layers can be directly visualized.

Moreover, from our research, most intrepretability research relating to KANs have been focused on smaller physically informed deep models, without much work on graph-based architectures. We hope that our work can contribute to the ongoing research surrounding interpretability of deep models, by demonstrating the potential of GKANs as a more interpretable alternative to GNNs for modeling spatial physical relationships.
